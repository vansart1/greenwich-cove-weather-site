The regional storm setup is active and serious. The NWS Upton forecast for this area shows a 90% chance of showers and thunderstorms from 11 AM through 2 PM — falling directly inside the 10:00–15:00 wind window. The main severe hazard per the AFD is damaging wind gusts.

The local protective mechanism — the Long Island Sound's cool water creating a stable marine layer that weakens incoming storms — is weaker than normal today. The air–water temperature gap is only 4°F (water near 70°F, air 73°F), well below the 10°F threshold needed to activate meaningful coastal storm suppression. The Sound effect is rated moderate at most today, not the strong protective effect seen on hotter days with cooler water.

The more concerning local factor is upper-level wind shear at 35.7 kt (westerly). Strong shear organizes storm updrafts into long-lived, structured cells. On days with even a borderline sea breeze present — and a weak one is active today — convective cells arriving at the sea-breeze convergence boundary can intensify rather than weaken. This punch-through scenario is why the local effect is rated amplifying: the cove is unlikely to escape the storm any better than inland areas, and could briefly experience intensified outflow at the convergence zone.

There is a notable disagreement between the deterministic HRRR model and the NWS/AFD consensus on timing. HRRR keeps CAPE near zero through 1 PM before building to 380–1,150 J/kg in the extended afternoon period. The NWS forecast calls for storms beginning at 11 AM. The AFD attributes the timing uncertainty to wildfire smoke suppressing surface heating this morning. The NWS mesoscale ensemble (HREF) is the basis for the 11 AM call and is the more reliable tool for this type of smoke-complicated setup — weight NWS timing over the HRRR point forecast on today's timing question.

What would change the call upward: any watch or warning issued by NWS Upton, or radar showing organized cells ≥45 dBZ within 25 miles approaching from the WSW–WNW (the dominant shear vector). What would push the risk lower: smoke remaining thick through noon, CAPE failing to build above 500 J/kg, and storms tracking south of the area along the I-95 corridor.
